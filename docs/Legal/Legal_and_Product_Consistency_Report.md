## 5. Risk Report

### High Risk

**1. Names are not encrypted at the application level (partially addressed — see Addendum; remains open as a scoped, understood limitation, not a false claim).**
Names (`first_name`/`last_name`) are plaintext everywhere in the schema. Unlike the earlier draft of this report claimed, this is **not** an oversight or an incomplete rollout — it is a direct consequence of `first_name`/`last_name` being used in live, DB-side `ILIKE` partial-text search (customer search, order search) across `src/services/customers/getCustomersList.ts`, `src/services/orders/searchOrders.ts`, and `src/services/orders/getOrdersList.ts`. AES-GCM ciphertext is opaque per-value (random IV), so it cannot support partial/substring matching at the database layer — encrypting names today, without first redesigning search, would silently break "find a customer by typing part of their name," a core staff-facing feature. The Privacy Policy now states this plainly and accurately rather than promising something that isn't true. See Addendum §B for options going forward.

**2. Data-residency claim in the Privacy Policy (confirmed, no longer a placeholder).**
Confirmed directly by the founder: the Supabase project is hosted in the EU, Ireland, region `eu-west-1`. Both documents now state this as fact.

### Medium Risk

**1. Trial-plan branch allowance — resolved by product decision, not a documentation fix (see Addendum §C).** The founder decided the product should grant 1 branch on trial, not 3, and changed `src/constants/plans.ts` accordingly; the Terms' pricing table no longer mentions branch counts at all for any plan, by explicit instruction, until branch limits are ready to be finalized and communicated.

**2. Retention/purge automation reads as active but is currently inert (reworded).** The daily cron job genuinely runs (`vercel.json`, `0 1 * * *`), but every retention window it depends on is `null`, so it purges nothing today. The prior Privacy Policy wording didn't distinguish "this mechanism exists" from "this mechanism is configured and running," which could mislead a reader (or the founder, months from now) into believing data is already being purged on a schedule. **Recommend treating filling in real retention numbers as a pre-launch blocker**, since the revised wording now explicitly commits to that being incomplete.

**3. Erasure does not reach every table holding personal data.** `anonymize_customer_tx`/`anonymize_employee_tx` deliberately do not touch `join_requests` or `pending_invites`, both of which can hold a prospective employee's plaintext name/email/phone indefinitely if a join request or invite is never resolved — these rows are only reachable by the still-inert bulk purge (see Medium Risk #2). This is a real, already-documented gap in the internal design doc (`docs/deletion_retention_plan.md` §8), not something invented for this report. **Not corrected in the Privacy Policy in this pass**, because the current text doesn't make an affirmative "erasure reaches every record" claim that this would contradict — flagging it here as a product/compliance gap worth closing (either by extending the erasure-fulfillment flow, or by making sure the bulk-purge retention windows, once set, genuinely cover these two tables).

**4. Refund-policy scope was ambiguous (corrected).** Before this pass, ToS §7 didn't make clear whether "Refunds" meant Rinsion's own subscription billing or the in-product feature laundries use to refund their own customers. A laundry reading a future filled-in refund policy could have mistaken it for a constraint on their own customer-facing refund tool. Now explicitly scoped to subscription fees.

**5. No disclosed limits on SMS delivery reliability (corrected).** SMS sending has no retry logic — a single failed API call is logged and dropped, with no user-facing indication and no resend. Neither document previously disclosed this, which is a latent dispute risk (e.g., a customer's pickup-code SMS silently fails to send, and nothing in the documents set expectations about delivery reliability either way). Both documents now include a short non-guarantee / no-automatic-retry disclosure.

**6. Undefined internal jargon in a customer-facing document (corrected).** ToS §4 referred to "Product A," an internal-roadmap term never defined in either document — a drafting inconsistency the audit's terminology-consistency check is specifically meant to catch. Replaced with plain language.

### Low Risk

**1. Version-label drift (corrected).** Both documents' body text said "DRAFT (v3)" while their filenames said "v4.1" — cosmetic, but exactly the kind of drift the consistency check exists to catch. Both now read "v4.1," matching their filenames.

**2. Typo in ToS §8 ("may be be processed") — corrected.**

**3. Stray double blank line in Privacy Policy before §8 — corrected.**

**4. "Branch" is used (in the ToS pricing table) but never defined, and the product today doesn't meaningfully enforce branch-level staff restriction — every laundry effectively operates on a single branch (`src/services/branches/getSoleBranchId.ts`'s own comment: "Branch selection is removed from the UI").** Not corrected, because neither document currently makes an affirmative claim about branch-level staff restrictions that this would contradict. Worth a one-line definition if/when multi-branch staff assignment ships.

**5. No MFA exists in the product.** Confirmed absent from the codebase. Neither document claims MFA is offered, so there was nothing to correct — noted here only for completeness, per the audit's verification checklist.

### §B. Why names aren't encrypted, and what "fixing" it actually involves

This is not a gap that can be closed the same way phone/email were. `first_name`/`last_name` are matched with live, database-side `ILIKE '%...%'` partial-text search in three places:

- `src/services/customers/getCustomersList.ts:47,49` — the customer search box.
- `src/services/orders/searchOrders.ts:52-53` — order search by customer name.
- `src/services/orders/getOrdersList.ts:40-41` — order list filtering by customer name.

AES-256-GCM (the scheme `fieldEncryption.ts` already uses for phone/email) produces opaque, non-deterministic ciphertext — a different value every time, by design, so it can't be substring-matched or sorted at the database layer. The phone-number equivalent of "search," `computeBlindIndex()`, only supports **exact-match** lookup (a deterministic HMAC), which works for "find this exact phone number" but not "find every customer whose name contains 'kwa'." Encrypting names today, without changing anything else, would silently break customer and order search for every laundry using the product.

Closing this gap for real requires picking one of a few real approaches, each with trade-offs — this is a product decision, not something to make unilaterally inside a documentation fix:

1. **Leave names as plaintext, accept it as a documented, bounded exception**, relying on Supabase's storage-level encryption, RLS tenant isolation, and the access controls already in place (this is what the revised Privacy Policy currently says, and it's an honest, defensible position — many production systems encrypt only the highest-sensitivity fields like phone/email/national ID, not display names).
2. **Build a searchable secondary index for names** — e.g., store a set of per-token or per-trigram blind indexes alongside the encrypted name, so "search contains 'kwa'" becomes a lookup against precomputed hash tokens instead of `ILIKE` on plaintext. Real engineering work: new columns, a backfill, and search-query changes in all three files above; trigram indexes also leak more pattern information than phone's single exact-match index, so the privacy benefit vs. phone-style encryption is smaller than it looks.
3. **Decrypt-and-filter in the application layer** instead of at the database — fetch candidate rows (e.g., by employee/laundry scope) and filter by decrypted name in Node. Works at small scale (which is where Rinsion is today) but doesn't scale to pagination/sorting over large customer lists without extra work, and moves cost from Postgres to the app.

**My recommendation:** given the product is pre-launch and small-scale, Option 3 is the least risky way to actually encrypt names without an open-ended redesign — but it does need someone to go through `getCustomersList.ts`, `searchOrders.ts`, and `getOrdersList.ts` and rework the query/filter logic, plus a backfill migration, so it's a real (if bounded) implementation task, not a config change. I did not implement this without your sign-off because it changes how search behaves and I don't know your tolerance for that trade-off (or your timeline) — say the word and I'll scope and build it.
